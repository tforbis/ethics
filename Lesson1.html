Instructional Design Analysis

Target Audience

Undergraduate business students or entry-level professionals. The learner likely views business as profit-centric and requires a paradigm shift towards stakeholder value.

Pedagogical Approach

Constructivist framework using Gagne's Nine Events of Instruction. The lesson moves from "Gaining Attention" (scandals) to "Stimulating Recall" (personal morals) to "Applying Knowledge" (case analysis).

Student Learning Objectives

Distinguish

Contrast personal morals with professional ethical frameworks using real-world scenarios.

Evaluate

Analyze the financial and reputational impacts of ethical decisions on business sustainability.

Synthesize

Apply Stakeholder Theory to identify and balance competing interests in business dilemmas.

Major Concepts & Core Terms

The Internal Compass: Morals

Morals are an individual’s internal beliefs about right and wrong. They are personal, subjective, and often rooted in culture, religion, or family upbringing.

  • Derived from personal conviction.
  • Consistent across environments.
  • Example: A personal belief in total honesty.

The Social System: Ethics

Ethics refers to rules provided by an external source—such as codes of conduct in workplaces or principles in religions.

  • Governed by professional bodies/society.
  • Can vary between contexts (Legal vs. Medical).
  • Example: A lawyer defending a client they know is guilty.

Moving Beyond Shareholders

While the Shareholder Primacy model argues that profit is the only social responsibility of business, Stakeholder Theory suggests that managers must serve all groups who have a "stake" in the company.

Employees
Safety, Wages, Respect
Customers
Quality, Privacy, Truth
Environment
Sustainability, Footprint
Government
Compliance, Taxes, Law

Analysis: The ROI of Ethics

Why Integrity is a Competitive Advantage

Ethical behavior is no longer just "nice to have"—it's a survival mechanism in a transparent, globalized economy.

Risk Management

Avoids the "ethical collapse" that led to the bankruptcy of Enron or the multi-billion dollar fines of Wells Fargo.

Social Capital

Builds deep trust with consumers who increasingly boycott brands based on labor practices or environmental damage.
